#DAY220 What is an error budget—and why it matters more than 100% uptime A Service Level Objective (SLO) is a target for how reliable your service should be. It&#x27;s not a vanity metric. It&#x27;s not 100%. It&#x27;s not aspirational. It&#x27;s a decision-making threshold.

If you say your SLO is 99.9% success rate, you&#x27;re also saying: "We accept up to 0.1% failure before we need to stop and fix things." That 0.1% is your error budget. It&#x27;s your margin for risk, change, and innovation. It tells you how much unreliability you can afford before the cost outweighs the benefit. This is not theory.

This is how teams decide whether to launch a new feature this week or fix a regression. Whether to refactor now or wait. Whether to freeze changes or push ahead. But here&#x27;s the catch: SLOs only work when they&#x27;re owned by everyone.

• Product managers must agree that poor reliability is worth addressing, even if it delays features.

• Developers must agree to pause or prioritize bugs when the error budget is spent.

• SREs must agree the targets are realistic—and defendable without burnout. Without shared commitment, your SLO is just a metric. Not a policy. Not a safeguard. Here&#x27;s how high-performing teams make it work:

1. Start simple: Define SLIs that matter to users—like availability or latency.

2. Set realistic SLOs: Not 100%. Just "good enough to build trust."

3. Track error budgets: Know when you&#x27;re overspending.

4. Create a response plan: What happens when budget runs out? Who acts?

5. Make it visible: Dashboards, alerts, and review meetings keep it alive.

6. Iterate with data: Tweak SLOs based on actual outcomes, not assumptions. SLOs are not a checkbox. They are how mature teams balance reliability with velocity. If your team still treats outages as "bad luck" and launches as "just ship it," maybe it&#x27;s time to ask: What&#x27;s our error budget—and who&#x27;s watching it?
