#DAY209 Is Jenkins Still Relevant Today? Let&#x27;s Talk About It. When Jenkins first emerged in 2011 (building on the Hudson project), it revolutionized CI/CD.

• Before Jenkins, automating builds and deployments was a manual, painful task.

• Jenkins popularized the concept of Continuous Integration, making it accessible to teams everywhere.

• It was open-source, customizable through plugins, and flexible enough to fit almost any environment — from startups to Fortune 500 companies. In many ways, Jenkins defined DevOps pipelines before "DevOps" even became a buzzword. Fast forward to today. The world has changed:

• Infrastructure has moved to the cloud.

• Workloads are increasingly containerized and Kubernetes-native.

• We expect elastic scaling, immutable deployments, and self-healing systems — things that weren&#x27;t the design priorities when Jenkins was born. While Jenkins is still powerful, it struggles with:

• Scaling pain: Managing Jenkins clusters at cloud-scale can be complex.

• Plugin sprawl: Too many plugins create fragility and maintenance nightmares.

• Modern developer experience: Tools like GitHub Actions, GitLab CI/CD, ArgoCD, and Tekton provide faster, lighter, and more integrated workflows — especially in cloud-native environments. So, is Jenkins dead? Not at all.

• It remains relevant in many organizations with large, customized legacy pipelines.

• It&#x27;s still hugely flexible and battle-tested.

• However, for new, cloud-native projects, many teams are choosing tools that are built for scale, speed, and Kubernetes from the ground up.
