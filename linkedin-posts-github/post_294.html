#Day155 Feelings Aren&#x27;t Facts: Handling Conflict Like a DevOps Incident Ever been in a meeting where someone dismissed data just because they felt things were different? Maybe they insisted a system was "slow" despite clear metrics showing optimal performance. People do this all the time—react emotionally instead of analyzing facts.

In DevOps, we don&#x27;t make decisions based on feelings; we rely on logs, monitoring, and root cause analysis. But in conflicts, people often expect emotions to carry the same weight as facts. Some Systems Just Don&#x27;t Integrate Think Splunk and ArgoCD—one is for log analytics, the other for GitOps deployments. They serve completely different functions, and forcing an integration would be pointless.

Yet, in conflicts, we try to force alignment with people who think differently, even when their mindset is fundamentally incompatible. Just because someone feels something is true doesn&#x27;t mean it is. #DebugBeforeReacting A major e-commerce company recently faced a Kubernetes outage before a big sale event. Leadership panicked, blaming Kubernetes, automation, and engineering decisions.

But when the dust settled, the issue was a simple misconfigured ArgoCD deployment. The facts were there all along—but emotions took over before anyone checked the logs. This happens in everyday conflicts, too: A workplace misunderstanding turns into an argument because someone assumes intent instead of seeking clarification.
