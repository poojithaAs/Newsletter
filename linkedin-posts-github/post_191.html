#DAY229 We&#x27;re ready to launch! Every time I hear that, I smile. Because I&#x27;ve learned that readiness isn&#x27;t about the code compiling or the CI pipeline being green. It&#x27;s about resilience. It&#x27;s about risk. It&#x27;s about asking what happens when things go wrong. Before I take the pager, I always ask these 3 non-negotiable questions:

1. How does it break?

2. How will I know it broke?

3. What do I do when it breaks? You&#x27;d be surprised how many features get deployed without answering these. In my experience as an SRE and DevOps engineer, I&#x27;ve seen two types of launches:

• The "move fast" launch: Everything is built for speed, features are shiny, but no one&#x27;s asked what happens during failure.

• The "production-ready" launch: Where failure modes are documented, alerts are meaningful, and rollback isn&#x27;t a panic button — it&#x27;s a planned action. If you want 99.99% uptime, you don&#x27;t just need tests . You need a culture that expects things to break and plans accordingly. What I&#x27;ve learned over the years:

• The goal isn&#x27;t perfection — it&#x27;s controlled risk.

• If your release process depends on tribal knowledge, it&#x27;s not a process — it&#x27;s a liability.

• Getting the same alert twice? That&#x27;s not an incident. That&#x27;s toil. And toil is the silent productivity killer most teams ignore. Real reliability is knowing that when something fails, you don&#x27;t need a hero. You need a system that recovers gracefully ideally, before a human even gets paged. That&#x27;s why launches shouldn&#x27;t just be feature-driven. They should be SRE-reviewed.
