I never realized how much trust impacts everything—until I read The Speed of Trust by Stephen M. R. Covey I always thought trust was just an abstract value, something nice to have in relationships and business. But this book made me see it differently. Trust is actually a currency—it speeds up decisions, lowers costs, and builds stronger teams. And when it&#x27;s missing? Everything slows down.

One story that really stuck with me was about Warren Buffer $23 billion deal with Wal-Mart. Instead of going through endless legal reviews and negotiations, the deal was sealed with just a handshake and finalized in less than a month. That&#x27;s the power of trust—it eliminates unnecessary friction and lets things move fast. On the flip side, I&#x27;ve seen what happens when trust is low.

Covey talks about how low-trust organizations pay a "tax"—in the form of slow approvals, excessive bureaucracy, and disengaged employees. And I&#x27;ve worked in places like that. Even simple decisions take weeks, because no one trusts each other to make the right call. The energy is heavy, and the work drags. One of my biggest takeaways from the book?

Trust isn&#x27;t just about character—it&#x27;s also about competence. You can be the nicest person in the room, but if you don&#x27;t deliver results, people won&#x27;t trust you. It made me think about how I show up in my own work. Am I following through on commitments? Am I creating an environment where people feel safe to take ownership?
