ID:7326797188221620225 #DAY220 What is an error budget—and why it matters more than 100% uptime A Service Level Objective (SLO) is a target for how reliable your service should be. It's not a vanity metric. It's not 100%. It's not aspirational. It's a decision-making threshold. If you say your SLO is 99.9% success rate, you're also saying: "We accept up to 0.1% failure before we need to stop and fix things." That 0.1% is your error budget. It's your margin for risk, change, and innovation. It tells you how much unreliability you can afford before the cost outweighs the benefit. This is not theory. This is how teams decide whether to launch a new feature this week or fix a regression. Whether to refactor now or wait. Whether to freeze changes or push ahead. But here's the catch: SLOs only work when they're owned by everyone. • Product managers must agree that poor reliability is worth addressing, even if it delays features. • Developers must agree to pause or prioritize bugs when the error budget is spent. • SREs must agree the targets are realistic—and defendable without burnout. Without shared commitment, your SLO is just a metric. Not a policy. Not a safeguard. Here's how high-performing teams make it work: 1. Start simple: Define SLIs that matter to users—like availability or latency. 2. Set realistic SLOs: Not 100%. Just "good enough to build trust." 3. Track error budgets: Know when you're overspending. 4. Create a response plan: What happens when budget runs out? Who acts? 5. Make it visible: Dashboards, alerts, and review meetings keep it alive. 6. Iterate with data: Tweak SLOs based on actual outcomes, not assumptions. SLOs are not a checkbox. They are how mature teams balance reliability with velocity. If your team still treats outages as "bad luck" and launches as "just ship it," maybe it's time to ask: What's our error budget—and who's watching it? #SRE #DevOps #SiteReliability #SLI #ErrorBudgets

But then I picked up a book on it .BPF Performance Tools by Brendan Gregg What is eBPF? It's like adding smart sensors inside the Linux kernel. You can trace what's happening inside your system without touching app code, restarting anything, or installing heavy agents. It's lightweight. Powerful. And surprisingly elegant. Why does it matter (especially for DevOps/SRE folks)? Because logs lie. Metrics lag. But eBPF? It shows you the truth From network issues to CPU spikes, From slow syscalls to suspicious behavior eBPF lets you observe, understand, and act with precision. How did I get started (without losing my mind)? • I stopped trying to "learn the kernel" and just focused on one use case • Played with bpftrace scripts to see live data from my own system • Used tools like Cilium and bcc to learn by doing • Most importantly: I allowed myself to be a beginner What I learned: You don't need to be a kernel wizard. You just need curiosity + patience. eBPF isn't about showing off. It's about finally having the tools to debug like a detective, not just a hopeful guesser. Start small. Break things. ID:7324041874481041408 #DAY214 You're Not Running terraform apply;You're Running terraform destroy on Someone's Confidence There's a difference between helping someone grow and making them feel small. We don't just run terraform destroy unless we really mean it. We use plan, review changes, double-check dependencies—and only then we apply. Why? Because we know the impact is irreversible. So why don't we treat feedback the same way? Telling someone, "This code is trash." or "You clearly don't know what you're doing." isn't apply. That's destroy. You might not see the damage, but it happens: Confidence erased. Trust broken. Growth paused. Better way? Run a mental terraform plan before you speak: • What's the intention behind this feedback? • Am I helping or just venting? • Is this safe to apply? Then give feedback like a clean deployment: • Clear. • Supportive. • Focused on improvement. • No downtime for their self-esteem. Because the goal isn't to be the smartest person in the room. It's to be the one others want to build with.
