Should You Really Switch to Snowflake?

View on LinkedIn

Snowflake is popular it's the "data cloud" that many leaders want. But before you move huge amounts of data or sign another software contract, ask yourself:

Do we really need Snowflake, or are our current tools like AWS or Azure good enough?

A good SRE doesn't just pick the newest tool. They think about reliability, cost, and purpose. Because sometimes "new" just means "more expensive."

Let's Get Real

If you're already using AWS or Azure, you already have strong built-in data tools. The question isn't whether Snowflake is powerful — it's whether it's the best fit for what you need.

AWS Built-In Tools

Tools: Redshift, Athena, Glue

Best For: Grouping data, business reports, and steady usage

Advantage: Works perfectly with other AWS services and your team already knows them

Azure Built-In Tools

Tools: Synapse Analytics, Data Lake

Best For: Connecting with Power BI and meeting data governance rules

Advantage: Works smoothly with all Microsoft tools

When to Stick with What You Have

Steady Work: Data questions are predictable month to month

All-in-One Cloud: You use only one cloud provider

Clear Costs: You prefer a fixed monthly bill

When Snowflake Makes Sense

Works Across Clouds: You need to share data across multiple cloud providers

No Server Worries: You want to focus on data, not infrastructure

Changing Workloads: Data volume fluctuates often

Strong Data Control: You need rollback, time travel, and detailed governance

Key Questions Before You Switch

  1. Does it help the business?

  2. How will it fit into our tech stack?

  3. What about costs and usage limits?

  4. Is it secure and compliant?

  5. How will we monitor it?

  6. How will we migrate and validate data?

Business Alignment

Who uses this data? Analysts, AI teams, or operations?

Are we moving from legacy systems?

Do we plan to share data across clouds? Is this driven by real goals or hype?

Integration & Architecture

Which Cloud? AWS, Azure, or GCP

Storage Layer: S3, Blob, or GCS

Data Movement: Airflow, DBT, or Fivetran

Live Data: Do you need streaming via Snowpipe?

Tip: Treat your setup as code. Manage Snowflake in Terraform and Git from day one.

Cost and Optimization

Snowflake's pay-as-you-go pricing can be efficient or expensive — it depends on setup and monitoring.

Start Small: Size warehouses based on actual need

Usage Pattern: Continuous or bursty workloads?

Auto Start/Stop: Enable to save 60–80% costs

FinOps: Define who watches costs and sets alerts

Spending Limits: Add daily or weekly caps

Security and Governance

Access: SSO, IAM, RBAC, PrivateLink

Data Protection: Mask and encrypt sensitive info

Compliance: GDPR, HIPAA, SOC 2

Auditing: Track all actions and classify data correctly

Reliability and Monitoring

Without clear SLOs and monitoring, Snowflake becomes a mystery box.

Define SLOs: Query speed, data freshness, pipeline timelines

Monitoring Tools: Datadog, Prometheus, or native dashboards

Failure Plans: Define actions for pipeline or query failures

Recovery: Use Time Travel and Fail-Safe and test restoration

Migration Plan

Step 1: Inventory all data and dependencies

Step 2: Start with non-critical data

Step 3: Run old and new systems in parallel

Step 4: Switch fully with rollback plan

Step 5: Optimize and document learnings

Always migrate gradually. Big moves cause big failures.

After Go-Live

Ownership: Who maintains Snowflake — SRE or DataOps?

Support: Who handles alerts and on-call?

Change Control: Who approves data or scaling changes?

Continuous Improvement: Review costs, performance, automation, and scripts regularly

Quick Decision Guide

The SRE View

Snowflake isn't always cheaper — it's less messy. It's reliability as a service for your data. Use it when complexity, not capacity, is your biggest problem.

Benefits

  • Simplifies operations — no patching or scaling headaches

  • Grows and shrinks easily with workloads

  • Shares live data across teams securely

Trade-offs

  • Higher cost if unmanaged

  • Less control over fine-tuning

  • Depends on vendor uptime

TLDR

Native Tools = You're in Control Choose AWS Redshift or Azure Synapse if you want lower cost, tight integration, and hands-on management.

Snowflake = Easy Mode Choose Snowflake if you need simplicity, multi-cloud support, and flexibility without managing servers.

The best data platform isn't the trendiest — it's the one that solves your real problems.
