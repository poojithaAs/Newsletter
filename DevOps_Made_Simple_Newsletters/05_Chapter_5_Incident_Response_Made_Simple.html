Why You Need Incident Response

Incidents are inevitable. But panic is optional.

A good response process can turn chaos into control. Instead of scrambling, your team acts with confidence.

"You don't rise to the occasion: you fall back on your training."

The key to strong incident response? A clear plan, regular practice, and shared understanding.

The 3 Cs of Incident Response

1. Coordinate Get the right people working together fast.

2. Communicate Keep everyone in the loop: teammates, leadership, and users.

3. Control Stay focused. Make decisions. Reduce confusion.

These three actions keep the response steady even under pressure.

Who Does What: Key Roles

Incident Commander (IC) Leads the response. Delegates roles. Owns the timeline.

Communications Lead (CL) Sends clear, timely updates to internal and external stakeholders.

Operations Lead (OL) Tackles the technical problem using the right tools and strategies.

➡️ The IC is your anchor. CL and OL support the mission. These roles scale with the incident.

Case Study 1: Google Home Outage

A bug in Google Assistant caused devices to ping the server 50x more often than expected. Instead of recognizing this as an incident, the rollout continued.

Even after error messages began appearing, the team stuck to standard bug tracking. Users across the U.S. faced major service issues over the weekend.

"Managed incidents are resolved faster."

The fix finally came when the quota was increased, but by then, the impact had already spread.

Lesson: Declare incidents early. Loop in the right people. Don't wait for heroics. have structure.

Case Study 2: GKE Cluster Failure

Early morning in Europe, new GKE clusters began failing. The team scrambled across time zones. Logs pointed to certificate signing issues, but the real problem was a corrupted Docker image.

For over 6 hours, over a dozen engineers tried to fix it. It wasn't until someone noticed DockerHub traffic behaving differently in the U.S. vs Europe that the root cause clicked.

"You don't need to know the root cause to mitigate. You just need to know where it lives."

A registry mirror with a clean image finally ended the outage.

Lesson: Assign roles fast. Escalate wide. Use blunt mitigation tools early, even without full understanding.

Case Study 3: Lightning Strikes Google DC

Lightning hit a Belgian datacenter four times in two minutes. While servers switched to backup power, disk trays didn't. VM storage was affected.

The Persistent Disk SRE team quickly declared a major incident. The IC assigned OL and CL roles. Power was restored. VMs were moved. New tools were created in real time to handle edge cases.

"The Incident Commander focused on what mattered most: impacted customers."

Only 0.000001% of data was lost and that too from active VMs.

Lesson: Clear leadership and preparation allowed Google to turn a freak event into a structured recovery.

Case Study 4: PagerDuty NTP Issue

PagerDuty's internal NTP servers began drifting. One by one, engineers noticed clock-related errors.

By the time a major incident was declared, multiple teams were already debugging. The IC gathered everyone on a call, rotated engineers every four hours, and followed clear runbooks.

"The stressful and communication intensive nature of the game forces players to cooperate."

Failure Friday drills and simulation games helped teams move fast and stay coordinated.

Lesson: Practice builds confidence. Drills make the response muscle real. You don't want to rehearse for the first time during a real outage.

Best Practices :

• Set roles and responsibilities

• Choose a single common channel

• Use status update templates

• Run drills from real incidents

• Review and learn from postmortems

Bonus: Empower on-calls to declare and escalate incidents without hesitation.

TL;DR

✅ Declare incidents early and structure the response

✅ Prioritize mitigation over perfection

✅ Assign IC, CL, and OL clearly

✅ Practice with simulations and drills

✅ Build tools like rollback and resource dashboards ahead of time
