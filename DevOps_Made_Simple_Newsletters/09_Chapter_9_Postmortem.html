And when something breaks, we don't cover it up : we write it down.

In this edition, we're diving deep into what makes a good postmortem culture and how to make it a strength rather than a chore.

If you've ever thought:

  • "We never learn from our mistakes."

  • "Why did this break again?"

  • "We had an outage. Now what?"

then you're not alone.

A great postmortem is not just about what happened but also what changed after. And that's what makes your systems stronger over time.

What's a Postmortem?

A postmortem is a document created after an incident to:

  • Understand what happened and why

  • Identify what failed in systems, process, or tools

  • Drive real change via action items

  • Communicate transparently with your teams

It's not a blame game. It's a learning tool.

"Engaging with toil typically means time diverted from creative problem-solving."

Case Study: All Satellite Machines Sent to Disk erase

What Happened?

In 2014, a misfired decommission workflow led to every single satellite machine at Google being wiped globally.

That meant:

  • Frontend queries dropped

  • Ads weren't served

  • Latency spikes in India, Australia, and New Zealand

Root Cause:

A bug in the turndown automation tool caused all machines (instead of just one) to be marked for decommission. Machines were mistakenly reassigned to the process a secure wipe.

Impact:

  • 60%+ of global queries dropped

  • Latency increase for two days

  • Revenue loss (exact value redacted)

  • 48 hours of non-stop recovery work

How Recovery Happened:

  • All traffic rerouted to core clusters

  • GFEs rebuilt manually by SREs

  • Automatic scripts were too slow

  • Core clusters had capacity, but satellite machines took 36 hours to rebuild

Lessons Learned:

  • "Run-once" workflows aren't safe if re-run manually

  • No rate-limits = mass destruction at speed

  • "Zombie state" was left when rollback wasn't clean

  • Postmortem was circulated across all engineering teams within a week

"Instead of hiding the disaster, they exposed every flaw—clearly, openly, and early."

What Makes a Good Postmortem

✅ Clarity

  • Key terms defined in a Glossary

  • Summarized Timeline of Events

✅ Depth

  • Explores technical + organizational failure points

  • Measures impact (user queries, latency, team fatigue)

✅ Actionable

  • Prioritized Action Items with owners and bug IDs

  • Categorized: Prevention, Emergency Response, Monitoring, Provisioning, Cleanup

✅ Blameless

  • Focus on what went wrong, not who

  • Encourages reporting without fear

✅ Prompt

  • Written within a week of incident closure

  • Data is fresh, lessons are timely

Tools & Templates: Making Postmortems Easier

📋 Templates

  • Google Docs templates pre-filled via automation

🧩 Tooling

  • "Requiem": stores, indexes, and analyzes postmortems

  • Auto-imports roles, timelines, impact, detection method, and root cause

✅ Checklist to Verify Quality:

  • Were impact and root causes fully assessed?

  • Are action items verified and assigned?

  • Was the postmortem shared org-wide?

Think of a postmortem as a letter to your future team.

🎮 Gamifying Postmortems

Yes, writing postmortems can actually be fun. Here's how:

🏆 Rewards

  • "FixIt" weeks where top contributors close the most postmortem action items

  • Peer bonuses and performance reviews for effective postmortems

🧠 Ownership

  • Authors become go-to experts

  • Present findings at org-wide retros or tech talks

📊️ Leaderboards & Dashboards

  • Track action item closure rates, incident frequency, resolution time

  • Highlight reliability gains in reports and promotions

"We turn reflection into competition and the result is stronger systems and stronger teams."

⚠️ Watch Out for These Culture Fails

  • "Not my outage = not my problem."

  • "We're blameless… except when we're not."

  • "We didn't have time to write it."

  • "This feels familiar." (Because it is. You didn't fix it.)

TL;DR: Postmortems Done Right

✅ Transparent

✅ Actionable

✅ Blameless

✅ Tracked

✅ Rewarded

"Postmortems are the foundation of learning. They're not about who failed they're about how we grow."
