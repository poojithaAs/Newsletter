What Is Incident Response, Really?

Fixing an incident is only half the job. Managing it, getting the right people aligned, communicating clearly, and staying in control is what defines a mature ops culture.

"Managing an incident means coordinating chaos, not just fixing bugs."

At Google, PagerDuty, and other tech giants, incident response isn't just about engineering: it's about orchestration.

Incident Management at Google

Google adapted the Incident Command System (ICS) from emergency services. It defines 3 main roles:

  • Incident Commander (IC): Leads the response. Delegates roles. Stays in control.

  • Ops Lead (OL): Executes mitigations. Works on fixes.

  • Comms Lead (CL): Updates stakeholders. Manages external/internal communication.

Everything flows through these roles; especially the IC. If it's not delegated, they're doing it.

"You don't rise to the occasion; you fall to your level of preparation."

The 3 Cs of Effective Incident Response

Whether at Google or PagerDuty, great responses rely on:

  1. Coordination : Who's doing what, when.

  2. Communication :Internally, externally, clearly.

  3. Control: Leadership, decision-making, accountability.

"If something fails in your response, it's probably one of the Cs."

Case Study 1: GKE CreateCluster Outage :

What happened: A critical Google Kubernetes Engine (GKE) component failed to create new clusters across Europe due to a corrupted Docker image.

How it unfolded:

  • 7:00 AM : Zara confirmed customer impact and declared an incident.

  • 8:45 AM : Il-Seong, a senior SRE, assumed IC and formalized the response structure.

  • 9:56 AM : Puanani and Victoria identified the root cause: a corrupted image in DockerHub mirrored to Europe.

  • 10:59 AM : Team tried rebuilding binaries and reconfiguring fetch paths.

  • 11:59 AM : Tugay and GCR SRE purged the corrupt image from the mirror, resolving the outage.

Fix: Instead of relying on DockerHub, engineers rerouted the cluster configuration to fetch images from Google Container Registry mirrors. Eventually

✅ What worked:

  • Clear IC handoff

  • Escalation protocol

  • Effective use of mirror fallback strategy

❌ Gaps:

  • Two-hour delay without a formal IC

  • Misdiagnosis and scattered communication

  • Lack of early generic mitigationsLesson: Don't wait to organize. Mitigate fast. You don't need full understanding to act.

"You don't need all the answers to mitigate: just know where the pain is."

Case Study 2: Lightning Strikes Datacenter (Timeline-Based)

what happened: Four lightning strikes near Google's Belgium datacenter caused a power anomaly that led to major storage access issues.

Timeline:

  • Mid-2015: Lightning strikes hit the local power grid near the datacenter: four times within two minutes.

  • Immediately: Backup generators kick in, but disk trays fail to switch to battery power on the 3rd and 4th strikes.

  • Shortly after: VMs lose access to persistent disks. Alerts trigger a major incident declaration.

  • Within the hour: Persistent Disk SRE assumes the IC role.

  • First responders: Restart all machines not hosting VMs using documented runbooks.

  • Mid-incident: Machines hosting VMs + affected disks require coordinated live migrations before reboot.

  • Live coordination: Persistent Disk and GCE SRE collaborate via a dedicated IC to reroute VMs.

  • Engineering taskforce: Writes custom tooling on the fly to automate safe migrations.

  • Throughout: Comms Lead updates internal stakeholders, external support teams, and affected customers.

Fix:

  • Power was safely restored from backup to grid.

  • Engineers rebooted clean machines and migrated affected VMs to stable hosts.

  • New tools were built in real-time to handle hybrid storage + VM failures.

✅ What worked:

  • Fast IC assignment and use of existing runbooks.

  • Dedicated Comms Lead coordinated multi-level updates.

  • Custom tooling created mid-incident for safe recovery.

❌ Gaps:

  • No standard playbook for edge-case: disk tray failure without VM host failure.

"When lightning strikes twice or four times, you better have practiced the drill."

Lesson: Define vague areas early. Keep communication flowing. Build tools ahead of time.

Case Study 3: PagerDuty NTP Clock Drift

What happened: PagerDuty's internal NTP servers drifted out of sync, causing widespread service clock errors.

How it unfolded:

  • 7:53 PM : SRE on-call alerted

  • 9:49 PM : Major incident declared

  • 9:55 PM : Full response team assembled

  • Engineers followed runbooks but clock drift persisted

  • Every 4 hours, responders rotated to stay fresh

  • 5:33 AM : On-call applied final config fix

  • 6:13 AM : All services validated as recovered

Fix: A configuration change was applied to the NTP servers to stabilize timekeeping. The change propagated and fixed time drift issues.

✅ What worked:

  • Dedicated Slack channel + call bridge

  • On-call rotation for mental stamina

  • Recorded calls and detailed logs

"Treating incident response like a living system keeps us resilient."

Lesson: Rotate leadership. Practice often. Create recovery muscle memory.

Best Practices to Steal

✅ Train responders

  • Simulations (Failure Friday, DiRT)

  • Rolecards, postmortems, drills

✅ Prepare before the pager rings

  • Incident templates

  • Role cards

  • Pre-agreed channels

✅ Create mitigation tools now

  • Rollbacks

  • Traffic drains

  • Feature toggles

✅ Run drills regularly

  • Google DiRT

  • PagerDuty Failure Friday

  • Wheel of Misfortune

"Being ready isn't a mindset. It's a habit."

TL;DR: Incident Response Is a Team Sport

  • Plan before the fire

  • Practice response as habit

  • Lean on the 3 Cs (Coordinate, Communicate, Control)

  • Don't rely on heroics

  • Build tools you hope to never use
