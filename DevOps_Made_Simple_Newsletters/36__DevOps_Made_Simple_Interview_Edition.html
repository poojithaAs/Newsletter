Coffee with Sara Baqla, M.S. From Teaching to Tech, and What Comes Next

By Poojitha A S | #DevOps #SRE #AI #WomenInTech #CareerStories

On August 19th, I spoke at the Dallas Developers Meetup :  A Night of Cloud, sharing thoughts on AI as a Service.

Soon after, I got a message:

"I enjoyed your talk and wanted to connect. Would you be open to a virtual coffee?"

That's how I met @Sara Baqla, M.S.

What started as a coffee chat quickly turned into a two-sided interview about fundamentals, resilience, creativity, and Sara's journey from teaching music and biology to becoming a full-stack developer.

And just a couple of days later on September 3rd her entire she was laid off. Suddenly, the resilience she spoke about wasn't theory. It was real.

Here's our conversation, unfiltered.

Q: In your talk, you mentioned PagerDuty AI. So even if this AI is fighting the bug or pipeline issue, how do you know the fix is good? Because I feel like AI sometimes produces bloated code. How do you future-proof it?

Poojitha: Good question. PagerDuty AI doesn't actually "fix" code, it predicts incidents. Earlier, if something broke, we'd get a ticket, then spend three to four hours combing through logs, dashboards, and monitoring tools to figure out the root cause. Now, PagerDuty AI can raise a ticket, say "this looks like a cluster issue" or "a node isn't responding," and route it to the right people automatically. It cuts incident response time by 75–80%. It's not perfect, but most of the time, its predictions are spot on.

Q: So will it actually give you the solution? Like, "here's the code to fix it"?

Poojitha: Not exactly. It won't drop a full solution. But it does highlight where to look for example, a pod is down, node affinity isn't working, or a container is unhealthy. Instead of digging through endless logs, it narrows your search and gives you possible fixes. Think of it as a very smart assistant.

Q: That feels like a lot of trust. You're just accepting its guidance?

Poojitha: It is about trust, but balanced trust. AI is a guide it accelerates incident response. But fundamentals still matter. At the end of the day, engineers validate and implement the fix.

Q: Do you personally use automation in your workflow? Any tools you recommend?

Poojitha: Oh yes. I started with shell scripts—the old-school way. Then came Lambda functions for serverless execution. And now, GitOps is everywhere. But here's my philosophy: tools change constantly. Fundamentals don't.

  • You need to understand Linux.

  • You need to know networking.

  • You need to get comfortable with containers.

If you don't, then every fancy tool is just a black box.

Q: I saw your newsletter. Do you pre-write or schedule your posts?

Poojitha: (laughs) I wish! My process is raw. I wake up at 5 AM every Friday, write down what I learned that week, and publish at 8:30 CST. No scheduling. No backlog. Just what I'm learning and living in that moment.

Q: That's brave. I want to blog too, but I keep overthinking.

Poojitha: Don't wait for perfect. My first Terraform post had mistakes, and people called me out. Instead of stopping, I corrected myself and learned faster. Learning in public compounds. Start messy.

Q: Your background is so unique—from biology and teaching to software. What was the "aha moment" that pushed you into tech?

Sara: As a kid, I built little websites for fun, but I never thought of it as a career. After teaching, I realized I wasn't fulfilled. I wanted something bigger, something creative and technical. Bootcamp gave me structure, deadlines, and accountability that's what finally pushed me into full-stack development.

Q: How has your teaching background shaped the way you work as a developer?

Sara: Teaching taught me patience. It taught me to explain complex concepts in simple terms. And it gave me conflict resolution skills. With students, you don't say "you're wrong." You ask, "why did you do it this way?" That same mindset helps with clients and teammates now.

Q: What was the hardest part of transitioning from education to tech?

Sara: The job hunt, definitely. In teaching, if you had a pulse and a résumé, you were hired. In tech, it's rejection after rejection. And remote work made it harder not having colleagues around to bounce ideas off. Communities like 100Devs kept me sane. They reminded me I wasn't alone in this.

Q: You've worked with Next.js, Vue.js, and CMS platforms. Which do you enjoy most?

Sara: At work, we build many Next.js apps connected to Prismic, which I really enjoy. I've also experimented with Sanity CMS and Craft CMS. It's interesting to see how each one tackles similar problems in different ways.

Q: What's a project you're most proud of?

Sara: A client landing page I built solo in just two weeks. It wasn't the most complex project, but I learned new UI libraries, delivered under pressure, and proved to myself that I could figure it out. That gave me confidence.

Q: Do you see yourself staying full-stack, or do you want to specialize?

Sara: For now, full-stack. I love building end-to-end. But I'm curious about cloud and DevOps. I'll probably explore more of that soon.

Q: Outside of coding, what inspires or recharges you?

Sara: Running. I did a half marathon in April. My two dogs keep me active. And fiction novelsthat's my reset.

Q: Which non-tech role taught you the most about yourself?

Sara: Teaching. It shaped my patience, resilience, and ability to explain things. Even though I left the classroom, those lessons come with me every day.

Reflections

Talking to Sara reminded me of a few truths:

  • Fundamentals are always the foundation.

  • Saying yes creates unexpected doors.

  • Resilience is built in transitions, not titles.

And then, just two days later, she was laid off.

But here's what matters: a layoff doesn't erase talent, curiosity, or drive. Sara is still the same builder, teacher, and community voice who carved her way into tech.

If your team values resilience, fundamentals-first thinking, and curiosity, Sara is someone you should know.

"You don't know what doors that could open." – Sara Baqla, M.S.
