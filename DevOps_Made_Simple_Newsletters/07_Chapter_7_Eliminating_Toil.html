Chapter 7: Eliminating Toil

View on LinkedIn

✍️ By Poojitha A S Adapted from the Google SRE Book, with examples from Google's own operations.

What's Draining Your Team? It's Probably Toil.

Imagine this: your engineers spend hours logging into servers to clear space, fix flaky alerts, or reboot failed hardware. Same problems. Same solutions. Day after day

That's toil: and it's the fastest way to kill innovation on any DevOps team.

"Toil encompasses the predictable and repetitive tasks associated with service maintenance."

Toil is manual, repetitive, automatable work that doesn't create long-term value. It grows with your infrastructure and quietly drains time, energy, and morale.

How to Spot Toil (Before It Eats Your Roadmap)

Ask yourself:

  • Is it manual? (SSH-ing into boxes?)

  • Is it repetitive? (Keeps happening?)

  • Is it automatable?

  • Does it add lasting value?

  • Is it reactive, not strategic?

  • Does it grow with your infra?

If you're nodding "yes" to most of these… you've got toil.

"Engaging with toil typically means time diverted from creative problem-solving."

Google's Approach: Cap It at 50%

Google SRE teams don't spend more than 50% of their time on operational work (including toil). The goal? Force prioritization. Protect engineering time. Scale sustainably.

Case Study 1: Killing Manual Repairs in Google Datacenters

Too many line card failures. Too many manual drains and reboots. Too many outages waiting to happen.

As Google's datacenter infrastructure expanded, the number of switches and connections grew rapidly. What was once a small team handling a few devices became a nightmare of thousands of hardware issues. These failures especially line cards were frequent, and fixing them was both error-prone and time-consuming.

The Saturn Phase (Old Way):

  1. Detect the failed device.

  2. An engineer logs in and manually assesses whether draining traffic is safe.

  3. If it is, they manually drain traffic from the switch.

  4. A datacenter technician physically replaces the faulty line card.

  5. The engineer then restores traffic to the switch.

This process was time-consuming and error-prone. It relied heavily on human judgment, which increased the risk of mistakes especially when done repeatedly across thousands of machines.

The Jupiter Phase (Automated):

  1. Automated systems detect the failed card.

  2. Risk assessments are performed programmatically.

  3. If it's safe, the system automatically drains traffic.

  4. A "strike policy" is in place: reboot the card the first time, and if it fails again, replace it.

  5. Only if both automated attempts fail does a technician get involved.

"Instead of the short-term endorphin hit of fixing those machines every morning, I now had the more cerebral pleasure of knowing that I'd fixed the problem properly."

By automating detection, drainage, and even early-stage recovery, Google saved enormous amounts of engineering time and reduced the chance of outages due to human error.

What Worked:

  • User interfaces that didn't require tribal knowledge, reducing onboarding time and mistakes.

  • Reusable, modular automation instead of fragile scripts.

  • Embracing "good enough" automation to ship faster, even if not perfect.

  • Building guardrails and risk checks into the system to prevent outages.

  • Planning for long-term support and evolution of the automation. because even the best tools need upkeep.

Case Study 2: Decommissioning Legacy Filer Systems (Moira Project)

Problem: Legacy NFS/CIFS storage (filers) were expensive, latency-sensitive, and full of toil (manual provisioning, ticket workflows, physical deployments).

Solution:

  • Used a tool called Moonwalk to track actual usage

  • Broke the problem down into phases

  • Built a self-service Moira portal to manage share retirement

  • Replaced filers with modern alternatives (Google Drive, Colossus, Git-on-Borg)

"Challenge assumptions and retire expensive business processes." "Rejecting toil... is often the simplest way to eliminate it."

Tactics Used:

  • Promote toil reduction as a feature

  • Encourage user migration with nudges

  • Build self-service tools

  • Start with partial automation + human-backed interfaces

  • Embrace uniformity for automation to thrive

Toil Elimination Playbook

1. Identify and Measure It

Track toil like any other metric: hours, tickets, alerts, repetitive actions.

2. Engineer It Out

Go upstream—fix the root cause, not just the effect.

3. Reject It

Ask: what if we just didn't do this anymore?

4. Use SLOs as Shields

Let minor failures slide if they're within budget. Don't gold-plate reliability.

5. Start Small

Pick easy wins. Build confidence. Snowball change.

6. Make It Self-Service

Give users APIs, web portals, or doc-based workflows.

7. Automate Safely

Use guardrails: timeouts, alerts, retry logic, rollback plans.

8. Think System-Wide

Don't shift toil to another team—eliminate it completely.

9. Get Buy-In

Show how toil slows delivery and burns teams out.

10. Iterate Based on Feedback

Treat automation like a product—collect user feedback, refine it over time.

TL;DR: Toil is Work That Shouldn't Be Yours

  • Repetitive, unscalable tasks don't belong in modern DevOps.

  • Google's strategy? Cap toil, track it, automate it, and kill it at the root.

  • Start small. Show wins. Iterate with feedback.

  • Automation is a long game—but it's how you scale without burning out.
