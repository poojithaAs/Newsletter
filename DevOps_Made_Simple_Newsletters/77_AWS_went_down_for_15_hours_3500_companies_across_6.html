Cloud outages are not rare anymore.

✍️ Poojitha A S | SRE | Observability Series

October 2025. A DNS failure inside Amazon Web Services US East 1 affected 4 million users. Platforms impacted: Netflix, Snapchat, DoorDash, Disney+, Amazon retail.

Three weeks later, Microsoft Azure Front Door failed. Microsoft Teams stopped. Microsoft Outlook is unstable. Admins locked out.

Two months later, Azure was down again for 10 hours due to a storage policy change.

Prediction from Forrester Research: At least two major multi-day cloud outages expected in 2026.

Average downtime cost: 9,000 dollars per minute.

The problem?

Most teams still make reliability decisions based on feeling.

The better way: Error Budget.

You already have one. You just do not see it.

If your uptime target is 99.9 percent, you are allowed 0.1 percent failure. That is about 43 minutes per month.

That time is not failure. That time is your budget. You choose how to spend it. Deployments. Experiments. Maintenance. Incidents.

How it changes behavior:

Instead of asking "Did we break something?" You ask, "How much budget did we use?"

Example:

Week 1 → 15 features shipped. 5 minutes downtime. Budget healthy.

Week 2 → 12 features shipped. 10 minutes downtime. Budget dropping.

Week 3 → 8 features. No incidents.

Week 4 → 25 minutes downtime. Budget almost gone.

Result: Feature freeze. Entire team focuses on stability.

No blame. Just numbers.

What most teams get wrong:

They aim for 100 percent uptime. That creates fear. Nobody wants to push changes. Perfection slows innovation.

What to do instead: Improve step by step.

If latency is 450 ms today, target 400 ms next. Then 300. Then 200. Honest goals win.

The Four Golden Signals

Before you set a budget, you need to know what to measure. Google's SRE team narrowed it down to four.

1. Latency: How fast is your service? Do not use averages. They hide the users who are struggling. Measure at the 95th percentile. That shows what most people actually experience.

2. Traffic: How many requests are you handling right now? This is your early warning for capacity problems and your compass for scaling.

3. Errors: What percentage of requests are failing? 0.1 percent → 43 minutes of downtime per month. 1 percent → 7 hours per month. 5 percent → 36 hours per month. Small numbers. Big consequences.

4. Saturation How full are your systems? CPU, memory, disk, connection pools. When these climb, problems are coming. This signal predicts failures before users feel them.

Track these four. You have a clear picture of system health.

Budget Rules:

Above 50 percent → Ship normally. 25 to 50 percent → Slow down. Stronger reviews. Below 25 percent → Feature freeze. Stability only. Zero → Stop everything. Fix system health first.

When incidents happen (they will), have a simple response plan:

Detect in 2 minutes. Understand the impact in 5 minutes. Contain within 30 minutes. Stabilize within 2 hours. Postmortem within 48 hours.

Action items must have owners and deadlines.

Culture matters.

Dashboards are not enough. SRE builds tools. Developers own uptime. Both share on call. Rotate weekly. Have backups. Remove noisy alerts. Fix root causes.

Where to start: This week.

Pick 3 critical services. Measure these four signals: response time, traffic, failure rate, and system capacity. Set realistic targets. Calculate your error budget.

Start there.

Bottom line:

You cannot stop outages. Not even the biggest cloud providers can.

But you can control how much risk you take.

An error budget turns reliability from a feeling into a number.

Stop guessing. Start measuring.
