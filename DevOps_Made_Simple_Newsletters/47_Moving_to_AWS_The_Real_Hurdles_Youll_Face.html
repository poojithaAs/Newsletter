Moving to AWS: The Real Hurdles You'll Face

View on LinkedIn

✍️ By Poojitha A S | 9 Years in DevOps & SRE

"Moving to the cloud isn't hard because of technology. It's hard because of what teams forget to plan for."

Why Companies Move to AWS

Organizations shift to AWS for speed, flexibility, and scalability—tired of waiting weeks for servers, dealing with outages, and maintaining hardware. But success isn't about just moving. It's about understanding your current setup, controlling costs, and modernizing with intent.

Faster setup. Easier scaling. Better focus on innovation.

Step 1 – Check Everything Before You Go

Audit every application, database, and background task in your environment. This discovery phase prevents expensive "lift-and-regret" moments later.

  • Critical systems: migrate first or early.

  • Obsolete systems: retire or consolidate.

  • Complex systems: move last, re-architect carefully.

A complete inventory saves months of troubleshooting after migration.

The "Lift and Regret" Problem

Many teams rehost everything "as-is," then realize they rebuilt their old problems in the cloud—now with a higher bill.

Avoid it by:

  • Starting small with measurable goals (cost, performance, scaling).

  • Capturing baseline metrics for comparison.

  • Treating the first project as an experiment, not a full migration.

Step 2 – Pick Your Path (The 7 R's)

AWS offers seven migration strategies. Use a combination that fits your pace and maturity.

Most teams progress gradually: Rehost → Replatform → Refactor.

Step 3 – Start with a Pilot

Choose a non-critical workload. Set up networking, IAM, and monitoring. Run a full test including rollback.

Use IaC (Terraform, Ansible, or CloudFormation) for repeatable environments. Document every step—the lessons become your migration playbook.

Step 4 – The Bill Shock

The first AWS invoice often surprises teams. Common reasons: over-sized servers, idle dev environments, untagged resources, and data-transfer fees.

Prevent it by:

  • Enabling budgets and alerts on day one.

  • Using Cost Explorer, CloudZero, or Kubecost for visibility.

  • Treating cost control as part of architecture design.

Cloud success is measured not by uptime alone—but by efficiency.

Step 5 – The Dependency Domino

Hidden links between apps can cause chain failures after migration. Hard-coded IPs, shared databases, or forgotten cron jobs often break silently.

Mitigate by:

  • Mapping dependencies with AWS Application Discovery Service or CloudEndure.

  • Running dual environments until traffic stabilizes.

  • Shifting traffic gradually using Route 53 or load balancers.

Step 6 – Governance and Compliance

AWS freedom can quickly turn into sprawl. Uncontrolled accounts, broad IAM roles, and missing audits are common pitfalls.

Set guardrails early:

  • AWS Control Tower for account baselines.

  • IAM least privilege with CloudTrail logging.

  • AWS Config and Security Hub for automated compliance checks.

  • Consistent naming and tagging for cost allocation.

Governance doesn't slow you down—it protects your speed.

Step 7 – The Skill Gap

Cloud migration often stalls because teams lack AWS fluency.

Bridge it by:

  • Investing in hands-on training and certifications.

  • Running game days to simulate failures.

  • Pairing experienced cloud engineers with on-prem SMEs.

  • Encouraging knowledge sharing through internal talks and guides.

Culture shifts faster when learning becomes part of the workflow.

Step 8 – Monitoring and Blind Spots

After migration, many teams assume "it's in AWS, so it's fine." Until latency spikes or costs climb.

Best practices:

  • Enable CloudWatch, Prometheus, or Datadog from day one.

  • Define SLOs and alert on breaches.

  • Use X-Ray or Jaeger for distributed tracing.

You can't manage what you don't monitor.

Step 9 – Don't Stop at Migration – Modernize

Migration is step one; modernization delivers the payoff.

  • Move workloads to containers (ECS/EKS) or serverless (Lambda).

  • Adopt managed services (RDS, DynamoDB, SQS).

  • Automate CI/CD (CodePipeline, GitLab CI, GitHub Actions).

  • Apply Infrastructure-as-Code everywhere.

  • Gradually refactor monoliths using the Strangler Fig pattern.

  • Continuously optimize cost and performance.

Modernization is where agility, resilience, and innovation begin.

Step 10 – Lessons from the Field

  • Skipping dependency mapping doubled fix time.

  • Ignoring cost tracking caused 30–40 percent overspend.

  • Cross-functional DevOps + FinOps + Security teams migrated faster.

  • Continuous learning outperformed one-time projects.

Success is less about tools and more about habits.

TL;DR

Migrating to AWS is a cultural, financial, and technical evolution.

  1. Start small and learn fast.

  2. Build protections early—governance, security, cost controls.

  3. Automate everything.

  4. Modernize continuously.

Moving is easy. Modernizing is mastery.
