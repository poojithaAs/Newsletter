🎙️ Interview Edition #2 Smiles, Servers and Staying Curious

View on LinkedIn

✍️ By Poojitha A S

"The tech is the easy part. It's the people side that's the challenge." – Aly Ibrahim

🎧 About This Edition

Some conversations don't just happen they linger. This was one of those moments that felt more like catching up with an old friend than conducting an interview.

Aly Ibrahim is a technologist with an impressive resume.AWS, Red Hat, Microsoft, but more than that, he's someone who brings clarity, calm, and genuine warmth to every room he enters. I first connected with him at a tech meetup and later had the chance to co-present with him. What stood out wasn't just the titles. it was his honesty, the way he sees people, and his ability to break down complex problems without ever sounding intimidating.

In this unscripted conversation, we covered everything from childhood coding memories to DevOps philosophy, from burnout to building community, from the messy middle of tech politics to the tools he swears by. What follows is our full conversation, detailed, unfiltered, and full of the good stuff.

This conversation with Aly was refreshingly candid and thoughtful. It wasn't scripted. It flowed with laughs, honesty, and deep technical insights that felt both personal and professional. From his early love for coding to leading modernization at Microsoft and AWS, Aly has worn multiple hats and stayed kind throughout.

1. What first inspired you to pursue a career in tech?

Aly: I actually started coding when I was six or seven. My mom bought me a computer instead of a gaming console. With no siblings and barely any internet, coding became how I interacted with the world. At that age, it wasn't about career or money. it was about curiosity and joy.

She noticed that spark and supported me early on. While other kids were playing video games, I was trying to understand how they were made. She enrolled me in courses where I learned basic programming before I even left elementary school. That shaped everything. Of course, when I got older and started asking for expensive gear, she reminded me of her early investment. So yeah money came later. But love came first.

2. You've worked at AWS, Red Hat, and now Microsoft. How did you prepare for those interviews?

Aly: It's rarely just about what you know today. These companies care about how you think, how you solve problems, and whether you're adaptable.

Yes, technical knowledge is important. But the way you approach problems matters more. I always invest in myself over time learning consistently, staying curious. That mindset shows up in interviews. It's the difference between someone who memorizes answers and someone who can grow with the role.

Big tech changes fast. Containers, Kubernetes, AI none of that was mainstream just a few years ago. If you're rigid, you get left behind. If you're flexible, you'll always have a path forward.

3. Was there ever a time you doubted yourself or had to reinvent?

Aly: Many times. I started as a developer, moved into sysadmin, then DevOps, and eventually platform engineering. I've always adapted based on what the industry needed and what I wanted to grow into.

At one point, I was working in telco. It was slow to adopt modern tools like containers. I realized if I stayed, I'd get stuck. So I left. I had to convince the next company that I could work with Kubernetes even before I had production experience. But I put in the effort, studied hard, and proved I could deliver.

The same thing happened with AI. I sensed the wave early and leaned in. Reinvention doesn't mean abandoning your past—it means building on it from a new angle.

4. What's one thing you love and one thing you dislike about working in big tech?

Aly: Ironically, the same thing: structure. I love the clarity in roles and responsibilities. It gives focus. But sometimes structure becomes territory. You try to help across domains and people think you're overstepping.

Culture matters. In great cultures, collaboration is welcomed. In rigid ones, people get defensive. I've seen both. The same structure can feel empowering or suffocating depending on how people treat each other.

5. How do you define Developer Experience in 2025?

Aly: It's all about reducing friction. Developers should focus on solving business problems not dealing with environments or broken pipelines.

Back in the day, DevEx meant giving them preconfigured containers or streamlined testing environments. Today, it includes things like GitHub Copilot, agent-based terminals, AI-assisted pipelines. A good DevEx platform is like an invisible productivity boost.

Give developers the tools to be 10x. Or watch them fall behind those who use them.

6. When starting a modernization effort, where do you begin?

Aly: Start with business value. What are the systems that drive revenue or customer experience? Don't modernize everything modernize what matters.

Technically, I look at databases, code structure, deploy pipelines, and legacy integrations. Can we offload to managed services? Can we remove friction?

Modernization without a business reason is just expensive busywork.

7. Multi-cloud and hybrid, how do you choose?

Aly: If it's greenfield, go cloud-native. Serverless. Managed services.

If it's legacy or compliance-heavy, look at existing contracts, hardware, skillsets. Don't fight what already exists. Augment it.

There's no universal answer. Context is everything.

8. Why do you stay so active in the open-source and community world?

Aly: Because I learn just as much as I share. Talking to people in meetups or conferences shows me new tools, pain points, and different ways of thinking.

It also keeps me grounded. If I only stayed in my company bubble, I'd miss half the industry shifts. Community is like cross-training for your brain and your heart.

Plus, that's how we met, right? 😊

9. How do you manage burnout and balance?

Aly: During my master's, I used to wake up at 4 AM to study or build projects. Now, not always that early but I still try to start my mornings quietly.

I've learned to pause when burnout hits. Take a walk. Listen to music on long drives. Watch a show. Recharge without guilt. You can't pour from an empty cup.

My priorities now are family, work, community in that order. And I show up best when I'm rested and recharged with my priorities clear and my energy aligned..

10. Does an SRE really need to code deeply?

Aly: You don't need to be a software architect, but yes—you need to code.

Everyone in tech should know basic scripting. Bash. Python. Maybe some Go. Think of it like Excel or PowerPoint, coding is a basic tool, not a specialty anymore.

If your org uses Java or Go, learn enough to read and contribute. It's not about titles, it's about being useful.

11. GitOps vs traditional CI/CD : what's your view?

Aly: GitOps is DevOps evolved. It gives you audit trails, versioned infra, reconciliation loops. Tools like ArgoCD and Atlantis bring order to chaos.

Traditional CI/CD works, but GitOps gives you confidence at scale.

12. What's your ideal DevOps stack in 2025?

Aly:

  • Cloud: Depends on the use case

  • Infra: Containers + Serverless

  • CI/CD: GitHub Actions + ArgoCD

  • IaC: Terraform (whether we like it or not)

  • Monitoring: Managed Prometheus

  • Security: Centralized golden images + policies

Keep it portable. Keep it sane.

13. Kubernetes is powerful but painful. What's the trap?

Aly: Treating it like a plug-and-play tool. It's not. It's an ecosystem. Without planning for RBAC, networking, observability, or scaling you'll end up firefighting every week.

Adopt Kubernetes only if you're ready to treat it like a platform not a tool.

14. Hardest challenge—technical or political?

Aly: Politics. 100%.

I've been on projects where dev, infra, and security teams had conflicting goals. Every change was a negotiation. As a consultant, I had to stay neutral, build trust with everyone, and show that I wasn't taking sides.

My background helped I've been a dev and an ops person, so I understand both languages. But yeah, people are harder than platforms.

15. What's the limit?

Aly: There's no limit except the ones you place on yourself. Learn deeply. Lead kindly. Rest when needed. Stay curious.

TL;DR

  • Coding isn't just for devs : it's survival

  • Reinvention isn't optional, it's inevitable

  • DevEx = frictionless, secure, AI-assisted

  • GitOps brings operational clarity

  • K8s = platform, not a shortcut

  • Burnout? Listen to it, don't ignore it

  • Community is the best career insurance
