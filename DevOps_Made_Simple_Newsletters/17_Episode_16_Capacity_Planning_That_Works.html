The Foundation of Reliability: Capacity Planning That Works

Capacity planning is a fundamental aspect of site reliability engineering. It ensures that systems perform as expected under normal and peak conditions, while also avoiding unnecessary resource costs.

In this edition, we explore:

  • What capacity planning involves

  • How Google approached it during the Gmail launch

  • Principles and practices that help teams make informed scaling decisions

🔍 What Is Capacity Planning?

Capacity planning is the practice of ensuring your systems have the right amount of resources to handle current and future demand.

It helps teams answer questions such as:

  • How much load is the system handling today?

  • What growth is expected in the upcoming quarter?

  • Is there enough buffer for unexpected spikes?

  • Are we managing infrastructure costs efficiently?

The objective is to maintain reliability without overprovisioning.

💡 Case Study: Gmail Launch

When Gmail first launched, access was limited through invites. This was not just a marketing strategy. it was part of a deliberate capacity plan.

Google SRE teams:

  • Released the service gradually to monitor usage patterns

  • Modeled user growth and traffic behavior

  • Added buffer capacity to handle unforeseen demand

  • Adjusted the pace of rollout based on performance observations

This approach ensured stability while the service scaled.

🧠 Capacity Planning Best Practices

  1. Plan quarterly Infrastructure needs evolve quickly. Frequent reviews ensure alignment.

  2. Include a safety margin Plan for peak usage and allocate a buffer of 30 to 50 percent.

  3. Collaborate across teams SRE, product, and infrastructure teams should plan together.

  4. Base forecasts on percentiles Use 95th percentile metrics to avoid designing around outliers.

  5. Balance efficiency and reliability Avoid both excessive resource usage and service disruptions.

  6. Measure unused capacity Track and optimize areas of waste without sacrificing stability.

📦 YouTube Release Planning

Before major music launches, YouTube prepares additional CDN capacity in relevant regions. This planning is based on past launch data, expected user demand, and scheduled release times. The goal is to ensure uninterrupted playback while preventing backend overload.

Common Pitfalls in Capacity Planning

  • ❌ Planning based on average usage → Always use P95 or P99 data. Averages lie.

  • ❌ No coordination with product teams → Feature rollouts often drive peak usage. Don't miss the signal.

  • ❌ Last-minute provisioning → Scaling takes time. Predict early, act early.

  • ❌ Ignoring background jobs → Batch processing and cron jobs add hidden load.

🔧️ Tools and Approaches

  • Prometheus and Grafana — For usage metrics and trend analysis

  • Autoscalers (Karpenter, HPA) — For dynamic resource scaling

  • Spreadsheets and capacity models — For structured forecasting

  • Load testing — For validating systems ahead of high demand

  • Chaos engineering — For identifying failure thresholds

✨ TL;DR: Make Capacity Planning a Core Discipline

  • Forecast based on observed usage and expected growth

  • Use buffers and staged rollouts to protect reliability

  • Align capacity with cost and performance goals

  • Schedule reviews regularly as part of your engineering cycle
