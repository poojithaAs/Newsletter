Who Is Sammy Cheung ?

I met Sammy Cheung at KCD Texas, wearing a KodeKloud t-shirt (a platform I've personally loved for years). What followed was one of the most candid, wisdom-filled conversations I've ever had.

Sammy isn't just a Cloud Ultra Pro, he's a force of mentorship, humility, and clarity in the tech world:

⚡ Quick Stats:

  • ✅ 101+ certifications in 6 years

  • ☁️ AWS Gold Jacket Ambassador, Azure & GCP Certified, K8s Kubestronaut, MCT

  • 🏋️ 30+ years in tech—Rockstar Games, Dexcom, Cloudreach, Breg

  • 🧑🏫 From Staff DBA → Cloud Architect → Instructor → Startup Founder

  • 🏆  Hiring manager turned career mentor for the next generation

Q1: Sammy, thank you so much for joining this session. I've been following your journey for a while now, and it's incredibly inspiring. You've completed over 100 certifications, stayed active in the community, and consistently show up online. Can we go back to the beginning.What first got you into tech, and what made you stay?

Sammy: Yes, so it's actually no brainer, because my father, he is an electrical engineer, so it's always good to follow the father's footsteps. So if you have at least one person you can ask.

However, I wasn't planned to get into computer science because I do not like programming. I like playing video games and putting things together. So naturally, I go to electrical engineering as a plan to go to college. But because I came to the United States to study, what happened is computer science was a shorter path to graduate. It's expensive. So I found the shortest path, and still going to do something.

And in hindsight, I think I picked a better path because technology, especially computer science grew much faster than electrical engineering, other than doing hardware.

And also because I have so many variations, like nowadays with AI, so this keeps me excited. And that's one of the reasons why I love to share with others, because when you're excited, you don't feel the pain. It's just all joy.

Q2: Let's talk about those 101 certifications. That's an amazing number. What got you started, and how did you keep going?

Sammy: Yes, sometimes people think getting certified on a piece of technology is hard. Actually, it's not that hard. It's kind of like I'm a marathon runner. When people say, "You run a marathon?" Which is, what, 26 miles? Or like 42 kilometers?

Just start with mile one. Or one kilometer.Same thing for technology. When I do certifications, I just want to learn something. Pick up something I liked. And then I say, "Maybe I can try another certification, See what's important these days and where I can apply that."

So instead of thinking about certification, I'm basically thinking: What new things can I learn that are related to technology?But nowadays it's not just about technology. It's: How can I apply it to business? Because technology without business is just research.Technology with business means real money, real career.As for how I retain what I learn that's part two.What I do is, not jump from one totally different technology to another. For example, if I learn AWS, I go learn what other cloud providers do which is similar. When you're doing something similar, you'll identify both the similarities and the differences.

So you only have to retain the core knowledge, and then just add the differences.That way, you can retain the core. What problems they solve.And sometimes I learn something I cannot retain, perfectly honest, not everything I study and clear the exam, I'm able to retain. But I retain some key concepts, and I can easily go back, restart, refresh, and improve.

Technology moves so fast. Something you don't remember? Doesn't matter. Maybe two years from now, no one uses it anyway.So you just need to know: "I learned that technology two years ago" enough to start a conversation or solve the problem. That's how I make sure it's applicable and I retain the knowledge.

💬 "Technology without business is just research. Technology with business means real money and real career."

Q3: I've always wondered, how do you manage everything? You work full-time, study regularly, show up to meetups, and still stay consistent on LinkedIn. What's your time management philosophy?

Sammy: Great question. I actually asked other people other influencers or successful people I know and they asked me the same thing: "How do you manage that?"

I said, "You do A, B, C, D, E, F, G, blah blah blah." They said, "You just described yourself."Then I realized: People look at us and say, "You do so much!" But instead, what I do is I have a core. For example, if I'm working, my core is working. If I'm studying, my core is studying.And when you study, you're definitely going to learn something maybe from a project that failed, or a project that succeeded. Just throw up something very simple. that becomes a content piece.

Because content is easy to create when you have experience. You just need to tell your story.And don't focus too much on polishing it. LinkedIn nowadays content is more important than polished content, if you know what I mean.Work, learning, and sharing are all related.When you learn something, that piece of technology will help you succeed at work. Because you may have a new project that uses it or you can recommend that solution.

So learning plus working equals project success. That becomes content.And one thing I will say is time management and setting boundaries that's something I still fail at.Sometimes I spend hours on LinkedIn, reading content but not producing content. So I try to limit it.Spend 5 to 10 minutes, share a post or article. If not, write a quick thought and hit Send.

Most of the time, I don't have time to come back and polish it. I'm not doing this professionally not yet, anyway.So this way, I learn faster, I help others, I stay consistent. Even if it's simple, just start today. That's your starting point.

🏆 "You don't need extra time. You need to combine purpose."

Q4: Was there ever a moment when you questioned this path? What helped you move forward?

Sammy: Yes. Multiple moments, actually. There were days I thought,"Why am I even doing this?" Especially when the job market was tough or when I didn't see immediate progress.But then I remembered why I started. My father's journey. My own curiosity. My love for learning. That's what brings me back.Also, community helps. When I see others share their stories, wins and struggles, it reminds me that I'm not alone. We're all figuring it out.

Q5: What does your daily routine look like with learning, work, content, and rest all packed in?

Sammy:

  • ☀️ Mornings: Light walk + podcast

  • 🧑💻 Work: Focus blocks, fewer meetings, take notes

  • 🌙 Evenings: Study, certs, or meetups

  • 🛏️ Weekends: Rest, reflect, recharge

Q7: You've built a massive following on LinkedIn. What's your strategy and what truly moves the needle?

Sammy: Be honest. Be consistent. Don't chase likes. Add value.You don't have to go viral. You just need to be real.Some of my most engaging posts were not planned. They were just reflections. A story from work. A failure I learned from. A certification I passed. Or even a joke about tech life.If people relate to you, they will follow. They will learn. And they'll remember you..

"You don't need to go viral. You just need to be real."

Q8: Do people need a large following to grow in their tech career? How has visibility helped you?

Sammy: No, you don't need 30,000 followers.But visibility helps especially in this digital age.When people see your posts, your projects, your growth they remember you. They recommend you. They DM you with opportunities.So even if just 10 people like your post, those 10 might include your next manager.

Q9: When someone feels overwhelmed by where to start cloud, DevOps, AI, code what do you suggest?

Sammy: Just pick one. Don't try to do everything.Pick a domain based on what excites you. If you like fixing things go with DevOps. If you like building maybe backend.Then go 10 levels deep before switching lanes.And give yourself 3 months. Learn one concept every week. Build one thing. Write one post. That's it.

Q10: How important are certifications in reality? What certifications actually matter?

Sammy: Certifications don't replace skills. But they show initiative.They help you learn fast, and they help recruiters filter candidates.What matters is: Can you solve problems?Some certs that matter: AWS, Azure, GCP, Kubernetes, Terraform.And one non-technical cert: Communication. Because soft skills make hard problems easier.

Q11: You've done over 100 certifications in just 6 years. You attend community events, post regularly, and have a full-time job. How do you find time for all that?

Sammy: It's not about time. It's about overlap.If I'm learning something for work, I take a cert for it. If I'm attending a meetup, I network and make friends. If I'm posting, it's something I learned yesterday.You don't need extra time you need to combine purpose.

Q12: I noticed you just share achievements and wins you're not a content creator in the traditional sense. Yet your presence is powerful. Why?

Sammy: Because people trust real stories. I don't try to sell anything. I just show up, share what I did, what I learned, and what I'm struggling with.If that helps someone, that's enough.

Q13: You've attended so many community events. What's your biggest takeaway from being part of the tech community?

Sammy: Energy. Positivity. People want to help you.You walk into a meetup not knowing anyone and walk out with new friends, collaborators, and ideas.Tech moves fast. Community helps you stay grounded.

Q14: What's your biggest learning from switching careers and entering tech later in life?

Sammy: You're not late. You're just in a different season.Someone might be ahead in years, but you might be ahead in passion or clarity.Everyone has a timeline. Don't compare. Run your race.

Q15: What's your biggest fear in tech?

Sammy: Becoming irrelevant.That's why I learn. I grow. I share. To stay sharp.But even if I lose relevance. I'll still help someone else rise.

Q16: How do you deal with burnout?

Sammy: By disconnecting. I log out. I walk. I laugh.Also by helping others. Teaching someone resets my energy.Burnout comes from overdoing things alone. Healing comes from sharing the load.

Q17: What advice would you give your younger self or someone just starting today?

Sammy: You don't need permission. You don't need to be perfect. Just start.Take one course. Write one line of code. Go to one event. Connect with one person.Do it scared. Do it tired. Do it now.

Q18: Final words for the DevOps Made Simple audience?

Sammy:Be kind. Be curious. Stay consistent.And remember your story matters. Share it.

Thank you, Sammy Cheung , for your honesty, humility, and heart. You've made DevOps feel more human.

TL;DR : Takeaways from Sammy Cheung

  • "Don't wait to be perfect. Just start."

  • Certifications are tools for learning—not replacements for skill.

  • Use parallel focus: Learn, apply, and share in the same flow.

  • You're not late—you're just in a different season.

  • Visibility = opportunity. Even if only 10 people see your post.
