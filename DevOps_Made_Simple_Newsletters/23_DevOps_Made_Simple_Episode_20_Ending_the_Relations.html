"Some breakups lead to breakthroughs." : SRE wisdom, learned the hard way

When SRE Teams Should Walk Away: How to End Well

Sometimes, the best move for an SRE team is to step back. Not because they failed, but because their work is done, or staying would slow things down.

Why SRE Work Should Finish

  • The system is stable and works well

  • The product is nearing its end

  • It no longer needs a lot of infrastructure help

  • SRE work becomes just routine tasks (toil)

A Normal Change

This isn't a failure, but a normal step in a project's life. SREs are brought in to fix big, complex problems, and their job changes as systems get better.

In this episode, we'll discuss why SRE work should end, how to do it smoothly, and what we learned from Google teams who gracefully exited their SRE roles.

Case Study 1: The Happy Breakup (Ares)

What happened: Google's Abuse SRE (Site Reliability Engineering) team was in charge of keeping CAT, an important company-wide tool, stable and running. CAT was the main system protecting Google from spam, phishing scams, and automated attacks.

But some problems started to appear:

  • New features for CAT were taking too long to develop, making it hard to fight fast-changing online threats like new viruses and account hacks.

  • The Abuse SRE team, following strict rules, focused on making sure the system was extremely stable and reliable. This meant lots of testing and slow, quarterly updates.

  • This clash between needing quick new features and the SRE team's need for stability caused big delays and internal arguments, slowing down the release of important tools to fight threats.

Solution: To fix this, a new, flexible internal development team called Ares was created within the CAT product group. The Abuse SRE team then taught the Ares engineers important SRE best practices. This included how to design strong systems, how to monitor things well, how to respond to problems, and how to manage large-scale systems. With this training, Ares started building and releasing smaller, separate software pieces and API-based modules that worked with CAT. This allowed for quick, repeated improvements and faster responses to threats.

Results:

  • CAT got new features much faster, moving from idea to being used in weeks instead of months.

  • Important new functions, which SRE's careful process had held up before, were now put into action directly by the Ares team. This allowed them to quickly adapt to new threats.

  • The Abuse SRE team had less daily work, especially for support and fixing urgent problems related to CAT's new features.

  • CAT became more independent, with Ares managing its own feature development.

  • Abuse SRE successfully moved into a role where they advised and oversaw things from a higher level, stepping back from the detailed daily work of CAT development.

Lesson: The best company changes happen when the team making the product is made stronger with the help and knowledge of the SRE team, not by trying to be agile despite SRE's rules.

Case Study 2: The Difficult Breakup (Data Pipeline)

What happened: For ten years, a big company data analysis system was managed by two different teams: a Product Engineering team that focused on new features and data insights, and an SRE team that made sure it stayed running and stable. Over this time, more and more data, different goals, and a constant lack of clear talks led to serious work and personal problems.

The Product Engineering team, without telling SRE clearly:

  • Started building a completely new, advanced data processing system to replace the old one.

  • Slowly moved important engineers and money away from maintaining and improving the existing system.

  • Developed totally different priorities based on the new platform's features and future plans.

On the other hand, the SRE team:

  • Kept putting a lot of effort and money into carefully keeping the old data system stable and performing well.

  • Didn't really know about the product team's changing focus and that the system they were supporting would soon be replaced.

  • Found it harder and harder to adjust to the product team's unspoken, changing priorities, which led to wasted resources and frustration.

The turning point: The working relationship reached a point where it couldn't be fixed. There was constant blaming, missed deadlines, and a complete loss of trust. Key people often found themselves caught in the middle of arguments between the teams.

In the end, the SRE team specifically assigned to this old data system was officially disbanded, and its members were moved to other teams.

The Product Engineering team then took full, complete responsibility for the entire data analysis system, including keeping it running.

Results:

  • At first, there were more system failures and problems in production as the product team got used to SRE duties. However, problems were fixed faster because they knew the system's design very well.

  • Roles and responsibilities became very clear, removing the old confusion and overlapping tasks.

  • The constant, tiring cycle of disagreements, power struggles, and blaming between the two teams completely stopped, leading to a more focused and efficient work environment.

Lesson: When a long-term partnership stops working and hurts company goals, a clear separation, even if difficult, can be better than continuing a cycle of arguments. Ending such a relationship can ultimately provide the clarity and focus needed for future progress.

TL;DR – When Should SRE Teams Step Back?

✅ The system works well and is stable.

✅ SRE's help isn't making a big difference anymore.

✅ The team is just managing daily tasks, not improving things.

✅ The product team can handle things on their own.

✅ Working together has become difficult.

Then it's time to smoothly give control back.

But do it with care:

  • Share what you know.

  • Plan for future support.

  • Let go without feeling bad.

Because when SRE steps back, it should still mean things run perfectly.

Best Practices to Break Up Like a Pro

Communicate clearly and often

Check value regularly (every 6-12 months)

Agree on goals and roles

Document everything for easy handoff

Focus on user experience and business value

If you're an SRE or DevOps engineer reading this, ask yourself:

Is your team still solving the right problems, or are they just doing things out of habit?

Knowing when to step back shows true engineering maturity.

The next time you feel growing stress or more hard work…

Remember: sometimes, a break leads to a new discovery.
