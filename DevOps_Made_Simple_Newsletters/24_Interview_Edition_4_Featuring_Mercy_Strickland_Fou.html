Introduction

I believe empathy is the most underrated leadership skill. And C3 is built on it without marketing, without ego, without asking for anything in return.

I didn't plan to write this interview. But after meeting Mercy Strickland through her husband David Strickland , whom I first met at the Dallas Developers Meetup, I joined a C3 newcomers session, and everything changed.

Mercy didn't smile to please. She didn't soften her words to win anyone over. She stood up, calmly and clearly, and said:

"This is a space for women to complement each other. We don't promote business, we don't discuss politics or religion, we don't gossip. If that's a dealbreaker, this isn't your space."

And I remember thinking this is not softness. This is protection. This is what it means to guard something sacred.

Real Stories from the Ground

I later joined the travel group, hiking group, fitness group where I met some of the most incredible women.

  • A single mom had to travel urgently for work, and Mercy personally took care of her child during her absence.

  • C3 stepped in to pay hospital bills, groceries, and rent when members were in crisis.

  • A cancer survivor who still hikes.

  • A woman who drove herself to the ER, alone.

  • A single mom finding her voice again.

  • A woman who sacrificed everything for her family, career, country, self, and was betrayed by the one she trusted most.

These stories didn't come with hashtags. They came with silence, strength, and healing.

C3 is not where you come to be saved. It's where you come to remember that you can stand back up. Even if someone just sits beside you until you're ready.

That's why I asked Mercy for this interview. And that's why she said yes, not for herself, but to remind others what it means to lead, quietly.

Interview with Mercy Strickland

Q1. C3 began as a simple gathering. What inspired you to start it especially at a time when you were still adjusting to a new country yourself?

Mercy: I'm basically from Bangalore, India. I moved to Dallas about five years ago. Before that, I lived in Scotland, London (Bournemouth)… I've always been around people. And when I came here, I didn't want to feel isolated. So I thought if I need community, maybe others do too. That's how it started.

Q2. Did you know back then that it would grow into something this impactful? Or did it evolve through small moments of connection and need?

Mercy: That was my hope all along. Dallas had a big influx of South Asians starting fresh, and I knew firsthand how overwhelming that could be. So I decided to use my own experiences to help other women adjust, connect, and build something truly meaningful together.

Q3. What core vision or belief has anchored C3 since day one and what has changed or deepened over time?

Mercy: Initially I was just planning to help new immigrants adjust to the US. Now we help people from cradle to grave, from dating to divorce, all aspects of life. Instead of just immigration, it's grown to everything.

Q4. You've supported women through everything from motherhood to trauma to reinvention. What does "healing through community" mean to you?

Mercy: For me, healing through community means not having to go through life alone. Sometimes all it takes is someone saying, "I've been there too" to remind you that you're not broken or alone.

Q5. Is there a story from within C3 that still moves you deeply, a transformation or breakthrough that you'll never forget?

Mercy: Oh, so many. There's one from yesterday. She moved to Dallas after marriage, couldn't work because of visa restrictions, had kids, then her marriage fell apart, she lost her visa, and now she's being forced to leave the country without her children because they are US citizens. This is the brutal reality so many immigrant women live with, and it breaks me. But then there are others—women who were completely broken and now they're the ones mentoring new members. That shift... that's everything.

Q6. How do you ensure that C3 continues to be a safe and inclusive space, even as it grows?

Mercy: One thing I do is meet new members in person every Sunday evening. It gives me a chance to get to know them and share the essence of the group along with the guidelines.

Q7. You've made yourself available to so many women. But how do you decide what's most important when everyone needs something?

Mercy: Honestly that's one of the hardest decisions of my position. I've ignored a phone call during lunch on Christmas day that I still regret. I've picked up phone calls at midnight that I also regret. But I keep moving forward impacting life one person at a time.

Q8. Isn't it emotionally exhausting to be available for others all the time? How do you care for your own mental and emotional energy? Mercy: Absolutely. But I've learned to take care of my own well being. I set boundaries, make time for things I love — prayer, journaling, working out, swimming, hanging out with family — and keep a few close friends who pour into me too.

Q9. When women bring their pain to you, how do you avoid absorbing it as your own? How do you let go while still helping?

Mercy: I'm able to help only because I absorb their pain as my own. I don't let go while still helping. That's what community does. They embrace pain and support one another.

Q10. Who do you turn to when you need support? What does rest look like for someone who gives so much? Mercy: My faith. My family. My routine. My C3 Leaders. When I watch our leaders taking charge and impacting so many women, that's my greatest rest.

Q11. Have there been moments you felt like giving up or doubted yourself as a leader? What kept you going?

Mercy: So many times. I've thought, "Why am I doing this?" But then I see a message, or a hug, or someone say, "C3 saved me." And I remember — it's not about me.

Q12. What do you wish people understood better about the emotional labor that goes into running women-led spaces?

Mercy: That it's not coffee and potlucks. It's deathbed calls. It's divorce. It's watching someone's life come back after abuse. It's holding space while they fall apart. That labor is invisible, but it's everything.

Q13. You spend 9 plus hours a day serving others. What sacrifices have you made for this mission that people might not see?

Mercy: I chose this path on purpose because it means so much to me, but it does come with sacrifices — missing time with loved ones, putting myself last, and carrying the heavy stories I hear long after the day is over.

Q14. Many smart women still doubt themselves, especially when stepping into tech, leadership, or visibility. Why do you think this happens?

Mercy: For ages, many of us — especially South Asian women — were raised to stay in the background and be humble, and some of that still continues today. On top of that, if you're dealing with immigration, all the paperwork, delays, and constant uncertainty can be so draining. It doesn't just take up time; it chips away at your confidence and makes you feel like you don't fully belong.

Q15. What's your message to women who are afraid to show up — on LinkedIn, in tech roles, or even in their own homes?

Mercy: Start small. Post once. Speak once. Ask once. And if it doesn't go perfect, that's okay. Courage is built one tiny risk at a time. You don't have to roar. Just whisper. That's enough.

Q16. How can communities like C3 help more women get comfortable with digital tools, online platforms, and tech opportunities without the fear of judgment?

Mercy: We already are. We have workshops, study pods, tech mentors. Some women come in not knowing how to use Zoom. Now they're learning Python. We just need patience, not pressure.

Q17. What advice would you give to a woman restarting her career after years away, especially if she feels like tech has moved on without her?

Mercy: She's not behind. She was building life in other ways. Start with one tool. One course. Ask for help. We'll sit with you. You're never too late for what's meant for you.

Q18. What impact do you hope C3 continues to make over the next 5 years, not in numbers, but in soul?

Mercy: In the next five years, I hope C3 continues to be a place where women don't just connect but truly feel like they belong. More than events or meetups, my biggest dream is to raise leaders who will go on to raise more leaders — women who take this same spirit of empathy, strength, and community into their own circles. That ripple effect is where the real, lasting impact will come from.

Q19. If you had to describe your leadership style in three words, what would they be and why?

Mercy: Walk with Purpose, Lead with Empathy, Empower others. That's true leadership.

Q20. What's one thing you want every woman in this world to remember about her worth, especially when the world tries to make her forget it?

Mercy: Your story matters. Good or bad, positive or negative. Whatever has happened in your life can help someone else. Don't erase your past. Leverage your past and use it to help others.

Q21. When your daughter, granddaughter, or another young woman reads your story one day, what legacy do you want her to carry forward?

Mercy: I want her to lead with empathy, to lift others as she climbs, and to believe in the quiet power of showing up for people. I want her to live boldly, love deeply, and lead with purpose.

TLDR – What Mercy and C3 Teach Us All

  • You don't need a big platform to change lives. Just a boundary, a heart, and consistency.

  • Healing doesn't happen overnight. But you're allowed to take up space while you do it.

  • No gossip. No business. No politics. No religion. Protect your peace with non-negotiables.

  • You can't pour from an empty cup. Leadership means knowing when to step back, too.

  • You don't save people. You sit beside them until they remember how to rise.

  • Asking for help is not a weakness. It's courage. And so is trusting others to walk with you.

  • C3 isn't about Mercy. It's about all the women who walked in broken and left whole — and stayed to hold space for the next.
